About The Workshop

People spend nearly one-third of their lives asleep and dream almost every night, yet sleep and dreaming remain underexplored within HCI. Beyond their critical role in supporting physical and mental health, sleep and dreaming also hold significant potential as sites for playful, meaningful, and technologically mediated experiences. Designing for these altered states of consciousness, however, presents unique challenges for HCI and necessitates interdisciplinary collaboration across neuroscience, design, engineering, and psychology. This workshop seeks to bring together researchers, designers, practitioners, and enthusiasts to critically examine current opportunities, challenges, and strategies for advancing SleepHCI. Building on prior efforts in this emerging area, the workshop will foreground empirically informed perspectives on how technologies can enrich sleep and dreaming experiences. By consolidating insights into a shared publication, we aim to establish a foundation for future research and design, further advancing HCI‚Äôs engagement with sleep, dreaming, and altered states of consciousness.

Call for Participation

We invite researchers, designers, practitioners, and enthusiasts to join us for a half-day workshop on Sleep and Dream Technology (SleepHCI) at CHI 2026. The workshop aims to bridge past learnings with future directions for sleep and dream interfaces by deriving design guidelines and research strategies. Specifically, we will explore how technologies can support, enrich, and reimagine experiences of sleep and dreaming for physical health, psychological benefits, creativity, and human flourishing. We will also examine the challenges, risks, and opportunities that arise when designing for altered states of consciousness. We welcome contributions from diverse disciplines, including (but not limited to): (1) Human-Computer Interaction and Design interaction techniques, somaesthetic design, speculative design, and participatory methods. (2)Neuroscience and Psychology: sleep science, dream research, mental health, memory, and learning. (3) Engineering and Computer Science: virtual reality, AI, sensing/actuating systems, and biosignal processing. (4) Arts and Humanities: creative practices, cultural perspectives, ethical reflection, and critical theory.

Participants are invited to submit a short position paper (2-4 pages in ACM format including references), a design provocation, or a case study that addresses one or more of the following themes: (1) Designing for sleep health, wellbeing, and rest. (2) Technologies for dream capture, representation, and sharing. (3) Ethical, cultural, and societal implications of sleep and dream technology. (4) Interdisciplinary methods for studying and designing sleep/dream technologies. (5) Speculative and artistic approaches to altered states of consciousness.
